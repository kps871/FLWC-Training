Troubleshooting — Symptom to Fix
How to use this table: Find what you hear in the left column. Go to the frequency range shown. Use a narrow boost to sweep and confirm the frequency, then cut. Work on the densest channel first — guitar, keys, or lead vocal.
What You Hear Frequency Range What To Do
Boomy, undefined low end 80–200 Hz High-pass all non-bass channels. Cut 100–150 Hz on the offending channel. Check whether kick and bass are competing in this range and carve space between them.
Muddy, cloudy, unclear — like a blanket over the mix 250–400 Hz Narrow cut on the densest channels — guitar, keys, piano. 2–4 dB of reduction here cleans up a mix faster than anything else. Rarely boost in this range.
Honky, nasal, or boxy quality on a vocal 400–800 Hz Boost-and-sweep narrow to find the exact frequency, then cut 3–5 dB. Common on close-mic'd vocals and acoustic guitar. The sweep reveals the worst offender precisely.
Vocal buried — present but not intelligible 1–3 kHz Small boost on the lead vocal at 1–2 kHz, or cut competing instruments (guitar, keys) in the same range. Cutting competitors is often more effective than boosting the vocal.
Harsh, grating, fatiguing over time 2–4 kHz Narrow cut at the specific harsh frequency. Check whether it is one channel or cumulative from many. The ear is most sensitive here — small adjustments make a large perceptual difference.
Sibilance — excessive "s," "t," "sh" sounds on a vocal 5–8 kHz Engage the X32 de-esser (Dynamics section, DE-ESS mode) on the vocal channel. Alternatively: narrow cut at the exact sibilant peak found by sweeping in this range.
Thin, no body — instrument sounds small 100–300 Hz Small broad boost in the low-mid on the thin channel. Also check whether the high-pass filter is set too high and is removing the fundamental tone of the instrument.
Dull, lifeless, no top end — mix feels closed in 8–12 kHz High shelf boost on overheads or acoustic sources. Do not boost here on close-mic'd instruments — it adds harshness, not air. This range opens up cymbal shimmer and room sense.
Kick drum has no punch — soft and undefined 60–80 Hz 2–4 kHz Boost 60–80 Hz for sub punch. Cut 300–400 Hz mud. Boost 2–4 kHz for beater click. Cut 500 Hz–1 kHz if the kick sounds hollow. Blend CH 1 and CH 8 (D6) for body + click.
Snare sounds boxy or hollow — no crack 400–600 Hz 5–8 kHz Cut 400–600 Hz to remove boxiness. Boost 5–8 kHz for crack and snap. Boost 150–200 Hz if the snare needs more body. The cut and the presence boost work together.
Bass guitar is muddy — all low end, no definition 250–400 Hz 700–900 Hz Cut 250–400 Hz mud. Boost 700–900 Hz for finger attack and string definition. Do not high-pass the bass — its fundamental lives below 80 Hz and you need it.
Guitar is too heavy — competing with kick and bass 100–200 Hz High-pass the guitar at 100–120 Hz. Guitar amps produce significant low-mid but no true bass. Removing sub-bass from the guitar clears space for kick and bass without affecting guitar tone.
Golden Rules
Rule 1
Subtractive before additive
Cut first. Remove what does not belong before adding anything. Most EQ problems are solved with cuts, not boosts.
Rule 2
Boost narrow to find, cut to fix
Use a narrow boost to sweep and identify the problem frequency precisely, then switch to a narrow cut. Do not guess.
Rule 3
HPF every channel that does not need low end
Vocals, guitars, keys, overheads — all carry low-frequency noise below their useful range. Cut it at the source before it accumulates.
Rule 4
Cut one instrument to help another
When a vocal is buried, cutting the competing guitar in the vocal's range is often more effective than boosting the vocal. Create space, do not fight for it.
