Compression Quick Reference

Starting settings for every source. GR meter diagnostics. The reasoning behind drums vs. vocals — not just the numbers.

Core Parameters
Threshold
The level at which compression begins
Signal below threshold is untouched. Signal above it is reduced. Start by lowering threshold until the GR meter just starts moving on average signal, then back off slightly.
Ratio
How aggressively the level is reduced above threshold
At 4:1 — for every 4 dB over threshold, only 1 dB passes through. Higher ratio = harder clamp. Limit (∞:1) = hard ceiling. Most live sources: 2:1–6:1.
Attack
How fast the compressor responds
Fast attack grabs transients — kills drum punch if too fast. Slow attack lets transients through before compression engages. Critical difference between drums and vocals.
Release
How quickly the compressor recovers
Too fast = pumping. Too slow = compressor never recovers between notes. Match release to the natural decay of the source. Auto release is a safe starting point.
Knee
Hard or soft onset of compression
Hard knee = compression begins abruptly at threshold. Soft knee = compression eases in gradually around threshold. Soft knee sounds more natural on vocals.
Makeup Gain
Restores level lost to compression
Compression reduces average level. Makeup gain brings it back. Set after threshold and ratio are dialed in. Do not use it to compensate for gain structure errors.
Starting Settings by Source
Source Ratio Attack Release Target GR Why
Kick Drum 4:1–6:1 5–10ms 50–100ms 4–8 dB Fast attack but not zero — let the beater transient through before compression clamps. Fast release recovers between hits so the compressor is not still working when the next beat arrives.
Snare Drum 4:1–6:1 5–10ms 80–120ms 4–6 dB Similar to kick. Tighten the crack without killing the snap. Slightly slower release than kick gives the snare tail room to breathe.
Overhead Mics 2:1–3:1 10–30ms Auto 2–4 dB Light touch only. Overheads carry the natural dynamic of the kit. Compress too hard and you lose the roominess and the natural swell of cymbals.
Bass Guitar 4:1 10–20ms Auto 4–8 dB Even out playing dynamics. Notes should feel consistent in the mix — not mechanically flat, but controlled. More compression here than on drums is appropriate.
Lead Vocal 2:1–3:1 15–25ms 100–200ms 3–6 dB Slower attack preserves natural dynamic expression. Catch the loudest peaks, not the performance. Soft knee sounds more natural. Longer release avoids pumping on lyric-heavy passages.
Background Vocals 3:1–4:1 10–15ms 100–150ms 4–8 dB BGVs need more consistency than leads — they should sit under the lead without poking through or disappearing. Slightly harder compression than lead vocal is appropriate.
Acoustic Guitar 2:1–3:1 10–20ms 150–250ms 3–5 dB Control dynamic pick attack without squashing the natural body. Slower release preserves the sustain of chord strums. Light-handed is almost always better here.
Sermon Headset 2:1–3:1 10–20ms 150–300ms 3–6 dB Spoken word varies widely in dynamics. Gentle compression keeps the pastor intelligible at both quiet intimate moments and emphatic peaks. Never crush the life out of a spoken performance.
GR Meter Diagnostics
GR meter never moves
Threshold is too high. Signal never reaches it. Lower the threshold until you see 2–3 dB of reduction on average signal levels.
GR meter permanently pinned
Threshold too low or ratio too high. The compressor is crushing the signal. Raise threshold or reduce ratio until GR peaks at 6–8 dB on the loudest moments only.
GR pumping in time with the music
Release time is too fast. The compressor fully recovers and slams down again on every beat. Increase release until the GR meter moves smoothly without surging.
Result sounds squashed — no life
Attack too fast or ratio too high. Slow the attack to let transients through, or reduce the ratio. For vocals — slow attack first. For drums — check ratio first.
GR moving 3–6 dB on peaks — sounds natural
This is the target state for most live sources. The compressor is working but the audience cannot hear it. Leave it and move on.
Vocal level inconsistent despite compression
The compressor is catching peaks but not the performance variation. Lower the threshold to increase overall compression, or ask the vocalist to maintain more consistent mic technique.
